How Does the Automatic Defrost System Work?

Every modern frost-free refrigerator — whether it is a 2024 LG French door or a 2010 Whirlpool top-mount — uses the same fundamental defrost cycle. The compressor and evaporator fan are turned off. The defrost heater (a glass or calrod element woven around the evaporator coil assembly) is energised and heats the coils to above 0°C, melting accumulated frost. The resulting water drains through a tube to the drip pan under the refrigerator, where it evaporates.

The defrost thermostat acts as a safety cutout — when the coil reaches approximately 10–13°C (50–55°F), it opens the heater circuit to prevent overheating the freezer compartment. On older models a mechanical timer (usually located in the control panel area) initiates defrost cycles every 8–12 hours. On all models made since approximately 2005, an adaptive defrost control board determines defrost frequency based on actual usage patterns — running defrost more frequently when the door is opened often, less frequently when the appliance sits unused.

What Are the Signs That the Defrost System Has Failed?

  • Fresh food section is warm, freezer stays cold: The most common presentation. The evaporator fan is blocked by ice, so cold air cannot circulate to the fresh food compartment. The freezer may still feel cold because the blocked coils are at the freezer level.
  • Loud humming or rattling from the freezer: The evaporator fan blade is hitting ice build-up on the coil cover. This is an early warning sign — if the noise recently started, the ice has only partially blocked the airflow.
  • Visible ice sheet on the freezer back wall: Remove the freezer back panel (held by 3–6 screws) and you will find the evaporator coils covered in a thick block of ice — sometimes 5–10cm thick after weeks of missed defrost cycles.
  • Water dripping into the fresh food section: As ice eventually melts from heat bleed from the freezer sides, meltwater overflows the drain tube and drips onto fresh food shelves. This produces a visible puddle in the bottom of the fridge.
  • Error codes: LG shows Er dH (defrost heater), Samsung shows 40C or 41C on French door models with defrost circuit fault detection.

Which Component Failed — Heater, Thermostat, or Board?

David K., our technician, follows a systematic component testing protocol rather than replacing parts speculatively:

1

Manual defrost and coil access

The freezer is manually defrosted using a heat gun or by unplugging the unit for 24 hours. This is required before testing — it is impossible to test a heater that is buried under 8cm of ice. The evaporator panel is removed to expose the coils and components.

2

Defrost heater continuity test

The heater terminals are disconnected and tested with a multimeter. A functional heater shows 15–30 ohms depending on wattage. An OL reading confirms the heater is open-circuit and must be replaced.

3

Defrost thermostat test

Tested at freezer temperature (it should be closed/continuity at -18°C). If the thermostat tests open when cold, it has failed in the open position — blocking the heater circuit permanently.

4

Defrost control / timer test

On mechanical timer models, the timer is manually advanced and voltage is measured at the heater terminals. On adaptive defrost control boards, the board's defrost output is tested in service mode by forcing a manual defrost cycle from the diagnostic menu.

5

Drain tube inspection

The defrost drain tube is checked for ice blockage (common in Toronto winters due to cold floors under the fridge). A blocked drain causes meltwater to back up and refreeze on the coils, creating a cycle of repeated freezing even after the heater is repaired.

Safety warning: Never use a sharp implement to chip ice off evaporator coils. The coils are thin aluminium tubes — a single puncture causes refrigerant loss, converting a $160 defrost heater repair into a $400+ refrigerant leak repair. Use a hair dryer on low heat held 15cm from the coils, or simply unplug the unit and wait.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why are my refrigerator evaporator coils frosted over?
Evaporator coils frost over because the automatic defrost system has failed to complete a defrost cycle. The defrost heater, defrost thermostat, defrost timer/control board, or defrost sensor has failed, leaving frost to accumulate until it completely blocks airflow over the coils — which stops cooling.
How does the automatic defrost system work in a refrigerator?
Modern frost-free refrigerators run a defrost cycle every 8–12 hours. The main board activates the defrost heater (a resistance element surrounding the evaporator coils) for 20–30 minutes, melting accumulated frost. The defrost thermostat cuts power when coil temperature reaches approximately 10°C to prevent overheating. Meltwater drains through a drain tube to the drip pan.
What are the signs of defrost system failure?
Key signs include: fresh food section warming while freezer stays cold (fan is blocked by ice), loud humming from the freezer area (fan blade hitting ice build-up), visible ice sheet on the freezer back wall, frost on food packages in the freezer, and water dripping from the freezer onto fresh food shelves.
